# Aroma360 — Customer Research Exercise


> 🔬 Brand: Aroma360 | Exercise 9: Customer Research

Research Methods: Brand website analysis (aroma360.com), competitor benchmarking (AromaTech, Pura, Scentiment), review sentiment analysis (Trustpilot, Reddit, PissedConsumer), commercial scenting industry research, and social listening across fragrance and home improvement communities.

Objective: Map the Aroma360 customer — from the home enthusiast to the commercial buyer — understanding dual-market positioning, purchase motivations, and creative white space.


# Customer Personas


## 1. "The Tech-Forward Homeowner" — Derek, 36

Demographics: Male, 30-42, $100K-$170K income, tech professional or adjacent. Lives in a smart-home-enabled apartment or townhouse in Seattle, San Jose, or Raleigh-Durham.

Psychographics: Approaches home improvement like system architecture. Every device needs to be optimized, integrated, and ideally controllable from his phone. He found Aroma360 while researching "best cold-air diffusion technology" — not "best candle." He cares about the engineering: waterless nebulizing tech, coverage area specs, oil consumption rates.

Motivations: Mastery over his environment. Scenting is another system to optimize. The Mini Pro's pod system appeals because it's modular and mess-free. He appreciates that A360 positions itself as a technology company that happens to sell fragrance, not the other way around.

Purchase behavior: Deep comparison research (A360 vs AromaTech vs Hotel Collection). Reads spec sheets. Buys the Mini Pro + Pro-Pod subscription to lock in the system. May upgrade to a Museum or DaVinci unit for larger spaces. Evaluates on performance metrics: sq ft coverage, oil longevity, noise level.

Pain points: Frustrated by inconsistent scent output. If the tech doesn't perform as specified, he'll leave detailed negative reviews. Wants better app integration and smart home connectivity.


## 2. "The Wellness-Conscious Mom" — Priya, 39

Demographics: Female, 33-45, $120K-$200K household income, suburban home, 2-3 kids, health-conscious lifestyle. Lives in a 3,000+ sq ft home in Plano, Alpharetta, or Irvine.

Psychographics: Everything that enters her home gets vetted — food, cleaning products, air quality. She's moved past Yankee Candle ("toxic paraffin wax") and Bath & Body Works ("synthetic everything"). Aroma360's cruelty-free, vegan, child/pet-safe positioning is exactly what she's looking for. Wellness isn't a trend for her — it's a lifestyle filter.

Motivations: Safety and purity. She wants her home to smell beautiful without compromising her family's health. Waterless cold-air diffusion = no water reservoir (no mold risk), no flame, no harmful particulates. She'll pay premium for peace of mind.

Purchase behavior: Finds A360 through wellness blogs, clean-home influencers, or mom group recommendations. Starts with Mini 360 SL for the main living area. Buys additional units as she expands scenting through the house. Loyal subscriber once trust is established.

Pain points: Needs more transparent ingredient lists. Wants to know exactly what's in the oil formulations. Subscription cancellation friction is a dealbreaker for this trust-sensitive customer.


## 3. "The Commercial Operator" — Victor, 52

Demographics: Male, 40-60, business owner or facilities manager. Runs a boutique hotel, spa, medical office, real estate staging company, or retail store. Based anywhere in the US.

Psychographics: Understands that scent is a business tool — it affects dwell time, perceived cleanliness, and brand recall. He's not buying fragrance; he's buying atmosphere as a service. He needs industrial-grade reliability, consistent performance, and bulk pricing.

Motivations: ROI on ambiance. Studies show scented environments increase customer satisfaction and spending. He needs a system that runs 8-12 hours daily without maintenance headaches. The DaVinci or Van Gogh large-format diffusers are built for this use case.

Purchase behavior: Comes in through the B2B/commercial scenting page or a sales rep. Evaluates based on coverage area, maintenance schedule, and total cost of ownership. May pilot one unit before rolling out across multiple locations. Negotiates pricing.

Pain points: Needs dedicated account management, not consumer-grade customer service. Concerned about diffuser reliability for always-on commercial use. Wants flexible billing and bulk oil pricing.


## 4. "The Aesthetic Minimalist" — Yuki, 31

Demographics: Female or non-binary, 26-38, $70K-$110K income, design-conscious urban dweller. Lives in a curated studio or 1BR in Portland, Brooklyn, or Chicago.

Psychographics: Every object in their space is intentional. They don't want a diffuser that looks like a diffuser — they want a design object that happens to scent their apartment. Aroma360's Museum series and Mini 360 SL's clean lines appeal on an aesthetic level before a functional one.

Motivations: Design coherence. The diffuser needs to complement their Noguchi lamp and Hay furniture, not compete with it. Secondary motivation is the sensory experience — they're building an immersive environment where sight, sound, and scent align.

Purchase behavior: Discovers through design blogs, Instagram interior accounts, or Pinterest. Makes purchase decision based largely on product photography and design appeal. Buys the Mini 360 SL in a neutral color. Experiments with scents seasonally.

Pain points: Limited color/finish options. Wants more premium materials (ceramic, wood, matte metal) rather than plastic. Sensitive to packaging — expects an unboxing experience that matches the product's design positioning.


## 5. "The Subscription Optimizer" — Carlos, 44

Demographics: Male, 38-52, $90K-$140K income, suburban homeowner, value-conscious but willing to pay for quality. Lives in Tampa, Phoenix, or Atlanta.

Psychographics: Loves a good system. He subscribes to everything optimized — meal kits, razor blades, coffee. A360's Mini Pro + Pro-Pod subscription model speaks his language: set it, forget it, enjoy it. He appreciates the locked-in savings and the convenience of auto-delivery.

Motivations: Convenience and value. He doesn't want to think about reordering oil — it should just show up. The subscription discount makes him feel smart. He tracks cost-per-month mentally and compares to candle spend.

Purchase behavior: Converts on the subscription offer page. The "diffuser at $49.95 with subscription" hooks him — it's the razor-and-blade model. Stays subscribed as long as the scent quality holds and the diffuser works. May pause but rarely fully cancels.

Pain points: If subscription cancellation is genuinely difficult (a known complaint), he'll become vocally negative. Expects flexible pause/skip/cancel options. Also sensitive to oil quality consistency — if month 4 smells different from month 1, trust erodes.

# Purchase Journey Map


## Awareness

- Instagram/Facebook ads — often featuring the Mini Pro subscription offer or the Mini 360 SL at $29.95
- "Best home diffuser" search queries — A360 shows up in listicles alongside Hotel Collection, AromaTech, and Pura
- Commercial/B2B outreach — trade shows, Google Ads for "commercial scenting solutions," referrals from property managers
- Cross-pollination with Hotel Collection — many customers discover A360 through HC or vice versa
- Wellness and clean-home influencer recommendations — cruelty-free/vegan/pet-safe positioning attracts this audience

## Consideration

- Comparison research: A360 vs AromaTech vs Hotel Collection — customers look at coverage area, oil system (pods vs bottles), and price per month
- Subscription math: "$49.95 diffuser + $XX/month for pods" — customers calculate total cost of ownership vs. buying candles
- Trust evaluation: Trustpilot and Reddit reviews are mixed — customers weigh positive product reviews against negative service experiences
- B2B buyers request demos, ask about coverage for specific square footage, and evaluate maintenance requirements
- Safety vetting: wellness-conscious buyers check for specific claims (cruelty-free certifications, ingredient transparency, VOC emissions)

## Purchase

- Subscription-driven entry: The discounted diffuser + oil subscription is the primary conversion mechanism
- One-time hardware purchase: Some customers buy the Mini 360 SL or Museum diffuser outright, avoiding subscription commitment
- B2B sales cycle: Longer decision process involving demos, quotes, and sometimes pilot programs before full rollout
- Sale events drive volume — 85% off promotions mirror Hotel Collection's approach

## Post-Purchase

- Immediate gratification: Waterless cold-air diffusion produces noticeable scent quickly. "6 million homes scented" social proof reinforces good decision
- Subscription management: Monthly oil pod deliveries. Satisfaction depends on consistent quality and easy management
- Expansion trigger: Happy customers buy additional units for bedrooms, offices. B2B customers roll out across locations
- Churn risk: Subscription cancellation difficulty is the #1 post-purchase complaint. Customers who feel trapped become vocal detractors
- Cross-sell: Perfume & body line, scent accents, larger diffusers for bigger spaces. The brand ecosystem encourages expansion

# Decision Drivers & Barriers


## Decision Drivers (Why They Buy)

1. Waterless cold-air technology — The core differentiator. No water, no heat, no residue. Pure fragrance delivery. Appeals to both tech and wellness buyers
1. Child/pet safety positioning — Cruelty-free, vegan, no open flame, no hot surfaces. Removes the guilt/worry factor for families
1. Dual consumer + commercial positioning — A360 is one of few brands that credibly serves both markets. The Museum/DaVinci/Van Gogh line bridges the gap
1. Pro-Pod subscription model — The convenience of pre-measured, easy-swap pods. No measuring, no spills, no guesswork
1. Scale of social proof — "Over 6 million homes scented" is a powerful trust signal. Volume implies reliability
1. Aggressive entry pricing — The $29.95-$49.95 diffuser with subscription creates an accessible on-ramp to premium scenting

## Barriers (Why They Don't Buy or Churn)

1. Subscription trap perception — The 3-month minimum commitment and difficult cancellation process is the dominant negative theme across all review platforms
1. Customer service responsiveness — "Can't get a hold of anyone," "emails go unanswered," "confirmed cancellation but still charged" are recurring complaints
1. Scent quality inconsistency — Some reviewers report oils smelling "watered down" or "sour," especially compared to competitors at similar price points
1. Diffuser reliability — Magnetic locks not working, inconsistent mist output, and units arriving damaged are reported pain points
1. Brand confusion with Hotel Collection — Some customers don't realize they're sister brands and get frustrated when issues with one affect trust in the other
1. B2B buyers need more sophisticated onboarding — Current experience is too consumer-grade for commercial decision-makers

# Emotional Triggers


### Control & Optimization

"I've dialed in my home environment." A360 customers (especially the tech-forward male segment) get satisfaction from optimizing another variable in their living space. Coverage area, scent intensity, scheduling — it's environmental engineering.


### Protection & Safety

"My home smells amazing AND it's safe for my kids and pets." The cruelty-free, vegan, waterless positioning triggers a protective instinct. This isn't just about smelling good — it's about being a responsible parent/pet owner who doesn't compromise on quality.


### Professional Pride

For B2B buyers: "My space feels polished and intentional." The scent is the invisible finishing touch that separates a good business from a great one. It signals attention to detail, care for the customer experience, and operational sophistication.


### Smart Spending

"I'm getting luxury for less." The subscription model and discounted hardware trigger the deal-hunter's dopamine. Customers mentally compare to Diptyque candles ($70 each) and feel savvy about the per-month cost of A360.


### Elevated Daily Life

"Walking into my own home feels like arriving at a resort." The daily micro-luxury of a beautifully scented home creates a compound emotional effect. Each day reinforces the purchase decision through small moments of pleasure.

# Language & Vocabulary


### How Customers Talk About Aroma360

- "Waterless" / "Nebulizing" / "Cold-air" — The technology is the category language. Customers who know these terms are already educated on why it matters
- "Coverage area" / "Sq ft" — Functional language. Customers evaluate diffusers like HVAC systems, not like candles
- "Pod system" / "Pro-Pod" — The refill mechanism language. Convenience and mess-free are key associations
- "Child/pet safe" / "Non-toxic" / "Clean fragrance" — Safety-first language from the wellness audience
- "Commercial grade" / "Business scenting" — B2B language that signals professional use cases
- "Subscription" / "Monthly plan" / "Cancel" — The subscription model dominates the conversation, for better and worse
- "Luxury scenting" / "Premium fragrance" — Aspirational positioning language used by happy customers
- "Whole-home scenting" — The aspiration to scent multiple rooms. Customers think in terms of total home coverage
- "Design piece" / "Looks like art" — For the Museum/DaVinci line, aesthetics are part of the value proposition

# Insights for Creative

1. Lead with the technology story, not just the scent. A360's cold-air nebulizing tech is genuinely differentiated. Show how it works — the science, the engineering, the "no water, no heat, no mess" reality. This appeals to the dominant male/tech audience
1. The safety angle is underexploited in creative. "Child and pet safe" is buried in bullet points — it should be hero messaging for the family audience. Show the toddler playing near the diffuser. Show the cat investigating. The implicit "this won't hurt them" is powerfully reassuring
1. B2B creative needs its own lane. Commercial buyers have different triggers — ROI, reliability, professional image. Create separate ad creative showing the spa, the real estate open house, the boutique hotel. Speak the language of business outcomes
1. The Museum/DaVinci/Van Gogh naming convention is a creative goldmine. These aren't just diffusers — they're named after artists and cultural icons. Creative can play in the "art meets technology" space: your home as a gallery, your scent as a masterpiece
1. Subscription value needs reframing. Instead of "$49.95 diffuser with commitment," position it as "luxury scenting for the price of two lattes a week." Make the ongoing cost feel trivial compared to the daily pleasure it delivers
1. The "6 million homes" stat is powerful but underlevered. That's a community, not just a number. Show the scale. User-generated content from 6 million homes is an infinite creative well — if the brand can activate it
1. Differentiate clearly from Hotel Collection. A360 is more tech, more wellness, more commercial. HC is more lifestyle, more nostalgia, more status. Creative should feel distinctly A360: cleaner, more modern, more systems-thinking
1. Address the subscription friction proactively. Creative that says "easy to pause, easy to cancel, easy to love" directly neutralizes the #1 barrier. Transparency builds trust faster than ignoring the elephant in the room
