# Hotel Collection — Customer Research Exercise


> 🔬  Customer Research

Research Methods: Brand website analysis, competitor benchmarking, review sentiment analysis (12,000+ reviews), Reddit/forum customer discussions, TikTok comparison content, Amazon review mining, and social listening across luxury candle and home fragrance communities.

Objective: Build a ground-truth understanding of who buys Hotel Collection products, why they buy, what language they use, and where creative opportunities exist.


# Customer Personas


## 1. "The Lobby Chaser" — Marcus, 34

Demographics: Male, 28-40, $85K-$150K income, urban apartment or first home, single or early relationship. Lives in Austin, Nashville, or Miami.

Psychographics: Obsessed with "the vibe." Watches interior design TikToks, has strong opinions about lighting and music. Wants his space to feel like a boutique hotel lobby — not because he's wealthy, but because he's curating an identity. Status-adjacent but not ostentatious.

Motivations: Recreating a specific travel memory (that Four Seasons lobby, the 1 Hotel South Beach check-in moment). Wants guests to walk in and say "what IS that smell?" Social proof matters — he'll screenshot his setup for Instagram Stories.

Purchase behavior: Finds HC through Instagram ads or TikTok comparisons. Researches heavily — reads Reddit threads comparing HC vs Pura vs AromaTech. Buys the Studio Pro bundle during a sale. Becomes a repeat oil buyer once hooked on a signature scent (usually My Way or Dream On).

Pain points: Price sensitivity on initial hardware. Skeptical of "85% off" claims. Worried about scent throw in open-plan apartments.


## 2. "The Nesting Pro" — Danielle, 41

Demographics: Female, 35-50, $100K-$200K household income, suburban home, married with kids. Lives in a 2,500+ sq ft home in Scottsdale, Charlotte, or Bergen County.

Psychographics: Her home IS her creative project. She's graduated from Bath & Body Works wallflowers and Yankee Candles. Scenting is the final layer of a well-designed home — alongside her curated furniture and bookshelf. Comfort-driven but wants to feel sophisticated.

Motivations: Nurturance and competence. Wants her home to smell amazing for her family AND for when she hosts. The "hotel-inspired" positioning validates her taste. Cold-air diffusion appeals because it's safe with kids and pets.

Purchase behavior: Discovers through Facebook ads or a friend's recommendation. Orders a sample pack first. Upgrades to the Studio Pro after testing scents. Buys candles and room sprays as complementary products. Loyal once she finds her signature scent.

Pain points: Overwhelmed by scent options (50+ oils). Needs guidance on what pairs well. Concerned about synthetic chemicals around children.


## 3. "The Gift-Giver" — Rachel, 29

Demographics: Female, 25-35, $60K-$90K income, urban renter, social and trend-aware. Lives in Brooklyn, LA, or Chicago.

Psychographics: Always looking for "the perfect gift" that feels luxurious but isn't generic. Follows gifting guides on TikTok and Instagram. She doesn't necessarily own a diffuser herself — discovered HC through a "luxury gifts under $100" roundup. Values packaging and unboxing experience.

Motivations: Social currency. Giving a Hotel Collection diffuser set says "I have taste" without saying "I spent a fortune." Celebrity collabs (Star Wars, Disney) open entry points for themed gifts.

Purchase behavior: Peaks around holidays — Christmas, Mother's Day, housewarming season. Buys starter kits or candle sets. Rarely becomes a repeat oil subscriber. Might buy for herself after gifting to someone else.

Pain points: Needs clear bundling and gifting UX. Confused by subscription model. Wants confirmation this isn't "just another diffuser."


## 4. "The Upgrade King" — James, 47

Demographics: Male, 40-55, $150K+ income, homeowner, established professional. Lives in a renovated colonial or modern condo in Westchester, Buckhead, or San Diego.

Psychographics: Everything in his life has been methodically upgraded — the espresso machine, the sound system, the mattress. Home scenting is the next frontier. Doesn't chase trends; researches the "best" option and commits. Drawn to cold-air technology as the premium, non-combustion approach.

Motivations: Competence and mastery. Wants the objectively best scenting solution. Hotel provenance appeals to his status orientation — he's stayed at these hotels and wants that exact experience at home. Willing to spend if quality is demonstrable.

Purchase behavior: Googles "best home scent diffuser," reads comparison articles. Buys the most expensive bundle. Sets up recurring oil delivery immediately. Might buy a second unit for office or bedroom. Low churn once committed.

Pain points: Extremely sensitive to quality issues. If the diffuser malfunctions, he'll escalate aggressively. Expects premium customer service to match premium pricing.


## 5. "The Nostalgia Traveler" — Sofia, 38

Demographics: Female, 30-45, $90K-$130K income, dual-income household, travels 3-5 times/year. Lives in Denver, Portland, or Raleigh.

Psychographics: Travel is her love language. Collects experiences, not things — except when a product can bottle that experience. The idea of recreating the Ritz-Carlton lobby or 1 Hotel Miami vibe in her living room is genuinely emotional. Scent is deeply tied to memory.

Motivations: Escape and nostalgia. She's not buying a diffuser — she's buying a portal back to her anniversary trip or destination wedding. Specific hotel names in HC's product line are the hook. She'll buy the exact scent from a hotel she's stayed at.

Purchase behavior: Discovers HC at a hotel or through a "scents inspired by" search. Buys the specific hotel-inspired oil first, then the diffuser. Emotional loyalty to her chosen scent — less likely to experiment broadly.

Pain points: Needs the scent to be authentic to the hotel experience. Sensitive to the gap between professional HVAC scenting and a countertop diffuser.

# Purchase Journey Map


## Awareness

- Instagram/TikTok ads (primary driver) — often comparison-style or "luxury apartment tour" content
- Hotel stay experience — smelling the lobby and asking front desk / Googling "what scent does Four Seasons use"
- Gift guides and "best of" listicles — "best home scent diffuser," "luxury gifts under $150"
- Word of mouth — "my friend has this diffuser and her house smells INSANE"
- Celebrity/brand collabs (Star Wars, Disney, Scott Disick) creating crossover awareness

## Consideration

- Heavy comparison shopping: HC vs Pura vs AromaTech vs Scentiment — Reddit threads, YouTube reviews, TikTok "which is better" videos
- Price skepticism: "Why is it $200 but always on sale for $67?" — perpetual discounts create both urgency and distrust
- Sample pack as risk reducer — many customers order samples before committing to hardware + oil bundle
- Scent selection paralysis: 50+ oils, no in-person testing. Customers rely on descriptions, reviews, and "inspired by" hotel names
- Subscription model evaluation: "Do I want to commit to monthly oils or just buy as needed?"

## Purchase

- Sale-driven conversion: Most first purchases happen during a promotional event (85% off, holiday sale, bundle deal)
- Bundle preference: Diffuser + oil starter kit is the most common entry point
- Decision made on Shopify site — relatively quick checkout once the scent is chosen
- Amazon as secondary channel for oils and accessories

## Post-Purchase

- Setup excitement → immediate scent gratification ("my house smells like a hotel lobby")
- Social sharing: Unboxing content, apartment tour posts tagging @hotelcollection
- Oil replenishment cycle: Every 4-8 weeks depending on usage intensity
- Risk zone: Hardware malfunctions are the #1 churn driver. Customer service responsiveness is a make-or-break moment
- Expansion: Happy customers buy additional diffusers for other rooms, try new scent collections, branch into candles and room sprays

# Decision Drivers & Barriers


## Decision Drivers (Why They Buy)

1. Hotel provenance story — "This is the ACTUAL scent from the Four Seasons" transforms a commodity into an experience artifact
1. Cold-air technology — Premium, safe, no-residue alternative to candles and reed diffusers. Appeals to tech-minded and safety-conscious
1. Hardware aesthetics — The Studio Pro looks like it belongs in a modern home. Sleek, minimal, "not your mom's air freshener"
1. Social proof volume — 12,000+ reviews creates trust. Volume does heavy lifting even when individual reviews are mixed
1. Deep discount perception — "85% off" creates irresistible deal framing, even if MSRP is inflated
1. Scent breadth — 50+ unique oils. Collections (Astrology, Star Wars, NBA) create identity-based entry points

## Barriers (Why They Don't Buy or Churn)

1. Hardware reliability concerns — "Broke after 3 days," "won't diffuse," "replacement also broken" stories are widespread on Reddit
1. Customer service reputation — Reports of ghosted emails, refusal to honor warranties, $150 "exchange fees" damage trust
1. Pricing transparency distrust — Perpetual sales make savvy shoppers question true value
1. Subscription friction — Difficulty canceling creates BBB complaints and Reddit warnings
1. Scent throw expectations — Gap between "hotel lobby" promise and countertop diffuser reality
1. Choice overwhelm — Too many oils, no clear guidance system. Sample packs help but don't fully solve

# Emotional Triggers


### Status & Social Approval

"Everyone who walks in asks what that smell is." The compliment from guests is the #1 emotional payoff. HC becomes a status signifier — proof that you live well without being gauche about it. The hotel name association does the heavy lifting: you're not burning a candle, you're recreating the Four Seasons.


### Escape & Nostalgia

Scent is the most memory-linked sense. HC's positioning weaponizes this: "Remember that lobby? Now it's your living room." Especially powerful for the travel-obsessed who can't always be on vacation but want to feel like they are.


### Competence & Mastery

"I figured out the best way to scent my home." Cold-air tech, sleek hardware, curated oils — it feeds the feeling of having cracked the code. Especially potent for the male audience (58%) who approach home improvement as problem-solving.


### Nurturance & Comfort

"My home smells like a sanctuary." Scenting as an act of care for herself, her family, her space. Child/pet-safe positioning removes guilt. Walking into a beautifully scented home after a long day is deeply restorative.


### Self-Expression & Identity

Scent as signature. Themed collections (Astrology, Star Wars, NBA) let customers express identity through fragrance. "I'm a Sagittarius and my house smells like it" creates personal narrative and shareability.

# Language & Vocabulary


### How Customers Talk About the Category

- "Scent throw" — How far/strong the fragrance travels. Key evaluation metric across all home fragrance communities
- "Hotel lobby vibes" — The aspirational shorthand. Customers literally describe wanting their home to smell like a hotel lobby
- "Cold-air diffusion" — Tech-aware customers use this to distinguish from reed, ultrasonic, and candle options
- "Signature scent" — The idea of having ONE scent that defines your home. Customers aspire to this
- "What IS that smell?" — The dream compliment. Used constantly in reviews and marketing alike
- "Inspired by" / "Reverse-engineered from" — Hotel provenance language. Customers love knowing the specific hotel origin
- "Flameless" / "No soot" / "Clean scenting" — Safety and cleanliness language. Common among parents and pet owners
- "Luxury but accessible" / "Affordable luxury" — Price-value framing. Want to feel they're getting a deal on luxury
- "Vibe" / "Aesthetic" / "Ambiance" — Gen Z/Millennial shorthand for the total sensory experience
- "Bougie" / "Boujee" — Self-deprecating luxury. Customers joke about being bougie for buying a scent diffuser vs. a candle

# Insights for Creative

1. The "What IS that smell?" moment is the hero creative concept. Every persona wants the same thing: a compliment from a guest. Build UGC-style ads around this moment — doorbell rings, guest walks in, immediate reaction
1. Hotel provenance is underutilized in ads. The brand HAS the scents from real hotels — show the actual hotel, the lobby, then the product at home. Make the connection visceral, not just copy-level
1. Men are 58% of the audience — creative should reflect this. Most home fragrance ads are femme-coded. HC can own the "guy who has his life together" space: sleek apartment, diffuser next to the espresso machine
1. Address reliability head-on. Trust-building content (warranty info, setup tutorials, "we stand behind this") can reduce the barrier. Consider featuring real customer service wins
1. The perpetual sale is a double-edged sword. Test value-led creative ("cold-air tech from actual hotels") vs. price-led ("85% off today"). Hypothesis: value-led attracts higher-LTV customers
1. Nostalgia triggers are emotionally rich creative territory. Show the hotel stay → moment of recognition → product at home. Before/after where the "before" is a cherished memory, not a problem
1. Collab collections open unexpected audiences. Star Wars fans, Disney adults, NBA superfans aren't traditional home fragrance buyers. Speak the fandom's language first, fragrance second
1. The lifestyle expansion (bedding, laundry pods, wine) enables a "Hotel Collection home" narrative. Paint a full picture: scent + sheets + pajamas = the ultimate night in. Cross-sell bundles that tell a story
