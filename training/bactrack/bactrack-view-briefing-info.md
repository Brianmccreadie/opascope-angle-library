1. Brand Overview
BACtrack is the #1 consumer breathalyzer brand in the US. Founded pre-2005, they built the personal breathalyzer category from nothing — taking technology previously reserved for law enforcement and making it pocket-sized for everyday people. ~30 employees, low-to-mid eight figures annual revenue.

Positioning: BACtrack is not selling a medical device or a safety tool. They are building a new category of personal technology — one that gives people real information about something they currently guess at. The competitive frame is not other breathalyzer brands; it's the status quo of "I feel fine" and the quiet avoidance of "I'd rather not know."

Core reframe: A breathalyzer is a speedometer for alcohol. Knowing your BAC should be just part of drinking.

Positioning statement: BACtrack makes the world's most accurate personal breathalyzers — the kind of technology that was previously reserved for law enforcement, now designed for anyone who drinks and wants to know where they stand.

3. Claims & Compliance
What you CAN say:

"Police-grade" / "evidential-grade" fuel cell sensor — this is factually accurate and the brand's strongest claim
"Matches devices costing seven times more" — proven in independent testing
Direct comparisons to Chinese knockoff breathalyzers (BACtrack buys and tests every Amazon bestseller internally)
Reference the Fox News comparison test (C6 vs. police breathalyzer)
Use press quotes (LA Times, NY Times, Car and Driver, USA Today)

What you CANNOT say:

Never use "safe" / "safety" / "danger" — triggers the law enforcement association
Never say "drunk," "intoxicated," "impaired" — clinical, stigmatizing, triggering
Avoid "responsible drinking" — sounds like a PSA disclaimer
Never use "should," "must," "need to" — preachy and paternalistic
Do not claim it's a medical device
Do not position as a DUI prevention tool (even though it can be)
Never fabricate statistics or invent testimonials

Platform-specific: No known Meta/Google policy flags on device ads. The device business runs clean — no restricted category issues.




4. Brand Voice & Guidelines
Brand Voice Framework (Z9, March 2026) — three pillars:
Quiet Confidence
State facts directly. Don't hedge with "we believe" when you know. Let performance speak — "Matches devices costing seven times more" beats listing specs. Never brag. The reader should feel like they discovered how good the product is, not like they were told.

Tone reference: The diploma on the wall, not someone telling you they went to Harvard.
Casual Intelligence
Talk like a person, not a product manual. Sixth-grade reading level. Short sentences. Plain words. Replace jargon with outcomes — "Xtend® Fuel Cell Sensor Technology" becomes "a sensor accurate enough for the pros." Use "you" more than "we." Questions are powerful: "Ever wonder what two glasses of wine actually does?"
Normalization Through Invitation
Never preach, warn, or shame. Treat breathalyzer use as something that's already normal. Frame as discovery, not recommendation — "You didn't know this existed" > "you should buy this." Show social situations (happy hour, dinner parties, tailgates). Avoid anything that sounds like a PSA.

The Party Test: Would someone reading this aloud at a dinner party make the table lean in with curiosity or lean back with discomfort? If anything other than lean-in, rewrite it.

Underlying tone: "Your smartest friend who also likes to drink." Not a doctor. Not a cop. A real person who happens to know a lot about alcohol and the body, enjoys going out, and bought one because they were curious.

Visual energy: Leonardo DiCaprio in The Great Gatsby — the champagne toast with fireworks. Celebratory. Knowing. Confident. Completely in control.

Best-performing caption ever (decade of testing): "A police-grade smartphone breathalyzer that can tell you when you'll be sober? Yup, that exists."

Words to USE: Breathalyzer, device, BACtrack / Know, see, check, find out / You, anyone who drinks, people / Accurate, precise, real numbers / Confidence, clarity, control

Words to AVOID: Revolutionary, innovative, cutting-edge / Should, must, need to / Drunk, intoxicated, impaired / Safe, safety, danger / Responsible drinking / Streamline, optimize, leverage




5. Top Personas
The Social Drinker (Primary — Largest TAM) Adults who drink regularly in social settings — dinner parties, happy hours, tailgates, wine tastings — and have genuinely wondered "how many is too many?" or "am I good to drive?" Most don't know personal breathalyzers exist. The awareness gap is the #1 barrier, followed by the identity barrier ("I'm not an alcoholic"). Creative must bypass both by triggering curiosity, not making a case for the product.

The Gift Buyer (Seasonal Spike) People shopping for unique, useful gifts — especially around holidays, Father's Day, bachelor/bachelorette parties. The product's novelty factor ("wait, that's a thing?") makes it a natural conversation starter and shareable gift. Price point ($70–$130) fits premium gift budgets.

The Data-Curious / Biohacker Health-conscious adults who track sleep, calories, heart rate, and want to add alcohol data to their self-knowledge stack. Attracted by the ZeroLine feature (know when you'll be at 0.00%) and the precision narrative. Less price-sensitive, more feature-driven.
